Colorado v. Barry Morphew

Suzanne Morphew vanished on Mother's Day, May 10, 2020 from her Salida, Colorado home. Her bicycle was staged near the road. Her husband Barry was charged in 2021 — then the case collapsed amid a discovery scandal. Suzanne's remains were found in a shallow clandestine grave in September 2023. Toxicology found BAM — a wildlife tranquilizer — in her bone marrow. A grand jury re-indicted Barry in June 2025. He was the only private citizen in the entire region with access to BAM. Trial begins October 13, 2026 in Alamosa County.

★ Citizen-investigation page. All allegations on this wall are pulled from publicly available filings, charging documents and on-the-record reporting. Barry Morphew is presumed innocent. Nothing here is a legal finding or determination of guilt.

★ Case Overview

Background

Mother's Day, the bike, and the only man with the tranquilizer.

Suzanne Morphew — 49, mother of two daughters — was reported missing at 5:46pm on Mother's Day, May 10, 2020 by a neighbor. The last known person to see her alive was her husband, Barry Morphew. Investigators arrived at the Morphew residence at 19057 Puma Path near Salida, Colorado. The house was locked. No forced entry. Her vehicle was in the garage. Her bike was missing.

The bike was found down a hillside off a dirt road between the house and Highway 50, less than a mile from home. Her helmet turned up four days later — 1.52 miles west of the Morphew home, nearly a mile from the bike. Staged.

Suzanne had been unhappy in the marriage for months. She confided in friends about plans to divorce Barry. She was having a secret affair with an old acquaintance, communicating daily over LinkedIn and WhatsApp. Her last electronic message to anyone was sent at 2:11pm on May 9, 2020 — thirty-two minutes before Barry Morphew returned to the home.

Barry was charged in May 2021 with first-degree murder. The case collapsed in April 2022 when a judge dismissed it over repeated Brady / discovery violations by the prosecution. Then in September 2023, Suzanne's remains were found in a shallow clandestine grave in Saguache County — and the toxicology changed everything. BAM was found in her bone marrow. A grand jury re-indicted Barry in June 2025. He pleaded not guilty at arraignment in January 2026.

★ The Indictment · What the Grand Jury Heard

Grand Jury · June 2025

Barry's alibi doesn't hold.

Barry told police he set his alarm for 4:30am, left the house at 5:00am on May 10 for a pre-planned work trip to Broomfield. He said Suzanne was asleep in bed. He said she planned to ride her bike that day.

His employee told a different story. They were supposed to drive to Broomfield together, and weren't supposed to leave until the evening of May 10. Instead, Barry left early without telling the employee, called at 11:18am to say he'd already gone. When the employee arrived in Broomfield, Barry hadn't brought the right tools and the job materials hadn't even been delivered yet.

Hotel and business cameras showed Barry was in the Broomfield area for nine hours and forty-five minutes. He spent less than thirty minutes at the actual jobsite. He was seen driving around to various locations, discarding unknown items in separate trashcans. He returned to his hotel room from 12:34pm to 5:55pm — meaning when he got the call about Suzanne being missing, he was in his hotel room, not at the jobsite like he told police.

Phone & Truck Blackouts

The phones go dark in sequence.

Barry's cell phone was turned off at 2:47pm on May 9 — just minutes after he arrived home at 2:43pm, right after Suzanne's last message to anyone. It came back on at 10:17pm. Then off again at 4:32am on May 10.

Suzanne's phone stopped connecting at 4:23am on May 10, 2020. It never connected again. Eight minutes later, Barry's phone went dark. Suzanne's phone was never found.

Barry's truck telematics tell the same story: activity at the residence until 9:52pm on May 9, then activity in the driveway from 3:25am to 3:49am on May 10. Then a complete gap from 3:49am to 8:10am — during which Barry was supposedly driving to Broomfield. When the telematics came back online at 8:10am, GPS showed he was already in Broomfield — but the gap itself is inconsistent with every other day of telematics data.

BAM · The Smoking Dart

A wildlife tranquilizer in her bones.

BAM is a patented premixed sedative cocktail: Butorphanol (pain reliever) + Azaperone (tranquilizer) + Medetomidine (animal sedative, a.k.a. "rhino tranq"). It's used exclusively by biologists and veterinarians for large animals. One kit = 11 milliliters. Requires a prescription from a veterinarian. Sold by one company in the United States.

The El Paso County coroner tested Suzanne's bone marrow for BAM. All three chemicals were present. The butorphanol metabolite was also present — meaning Suzanne metabolized the drug before she died, ruling out postmortem contamination. Cause of death: "Homicide by unspecified means in the setting of butorphanol, azaperone, and medetomidine intoxication."

A pharmacology expert testified that BAM must be injected (IV or intramuscular — skin and GI tract don't absorb it). It's not FDA-approved for humans. No scientific studies exist on its effects in people. The expert testified it would render the recipient "wholly immobile, vulnerable, and unable to resist any constrictions to the recipient's ability to breathe."

Prescription records for the entire region, 2017–2020: the only entities with BAM were Colorado Parks and Wildlife and the National Park Service. Barry obtained his prescriptions in Indiana in March 2018 as a deer breeder, purchasing multiple kits. When Suzanne disappeared, only one private citizen in that entire area of the state had access to BAM: Barry Morphew.

★ Verdict Watch

Status · Trial Oct 13, 2026

Round Two. And this time there's a body with BAM in it.

DA Anne E. Kelly and Special Deputy DA Fred Johnson have what the first prosecution never did: Suzanne's remains, BAM in her bone marrow, a butorphanol metabolite proving she was alive when injected, and prescription records showing Barry was the only civilian in the region with access to the drug. The phone blackout timeline. The telematics gap. The needle cap in the dryer. The Broomfield alibi that cameras shredded.

Defense attorney David Beller's counter: the lab is the weak link. Only one of three labs found BAM. El Paso County Coroner's Office is certified for DUI blood-alcohol testing, not exotic veterinary compound analysis. If the Daubert challenge lands and the BAM toxicology gets excluded, the state's case loses its centerpiece. That's the whole trial in one motion.

31 additional defense motions pending as of May 2026 — seeking to exclude witness statements like "looked guilty." Next hearing June 24. Trial set for October 13, 2026, Alamosa County.
