Story 01
Story 01 · Flagg v. Swift

33 pages. One day early.

Swift's legal team dropped a motion to dismiss the day before the PI hearing — and Flagg's lawyer called them out for violating the meet-and-confer rule. Two tracks. Two hearings. One case that could be over before it starts.

★ may 26 · doc 44 · motion to dismiss

The MTD breakdown.

Filed May 26 via Venable LLP (Max N. Wellman, J. Douglas Baldridge, Katherine Wright Morrone). Two prongs:

Prong 1 · Rule 12(b)(6)

Failure to state a claim — all 4 defendants. "Shotgun pleading" lumps Swift, TASRM, UMG, Bravado together 90+ times. No likelihood of confusion — completely different goods/services. USPTO refusal was a non-final office action. UCL can't apply extraterritorially to a Nevada plaintiff.

Prong 2 · Rule 12(b)(2)

No personal jurisdiction — Swift + TASRM only. Swift domiciled in Tennessee (sworn declaration). TASRM is a Tennessee LLC. Property in CA doesn't = general jurisdiction. Calder effects test not met. Online store alone isn't "express aiming."

Rogers v. Grimaldi First Amendment defense reserved for later. Footnote: Flagg has no current performances, book unavailable, no podcast episodes.

★ may 26 · doc 45 · noncompliance notice

Flagg fires back same day.

Jaymie Parkkinen (plaintiff's counsel) files a 2-page notice calling out Swift's team for violating Local Rule 7-3 — the meet-and-confer rule.

The timeline problem
May 22Parties conferred (meet-and-confer)
May 29Earliest compliant filing date (7 days later)
May 26Swift files MTD — 3 days too early
May 27PI hearing (the reason they rushed it)
Jun 2Response deadline (they beat it by a week)

Swift's own counsel acknowledged filing early so the court would "have the benefit of the Motion to Dismiss prior to" the PI hearing. Flagg reserves all rights including right to amend under FRCP 15.

★ may 27 · downtown LA · judge serena r. murillo

The PI hearing.

Judge Murillo opened the hearing by zeroing in on what she called “the real crucible” of the case: should The Life of a Showgirl get First Amendment protection as an expressive work under the Rogers test? No ruling from the bench — she said a written decision would come “shortly.”

Swift · Doug Baldridge · Venable LLP

“This is the rare and special circumstance of an album — a classic expressive work. That’s why the First Amendment applies here. That’s why you can’t get a preliminary injunction.”

Called confusion between a Vegas cabaret at dinner venues and Swift’s album “not realistic” — “you don’t need to suspend your common sense.”

Cited Lady Gaga defeating a similar injunction over Mayhem album.

Said Wade waited 8 months after the album dropped to sue, and “glommed onto” the album’s commercial success. Noted Wade’s social media used Swift-related hashtags: “I don’t know if your honor follows Ms. Swift, but ‘era’ is a pretty big word for us.”

Flagg · Jaymie Parkkinen · Santa Monica

“That’s reverse confusion. She owns the mark, and defendant’s conduct is eroding it. Money cannot fix that.”

Argued the album title lost First Amendment protection when Swift applied to register it as a trademark at the USPTO — turning it from art into a branding campaign.

Pointed out the USPTO already rejected Swift’s application, finding likelihood of confusion with Wade’s mark.

Wade and Swift are “closely aligned — solo female performers operating in identical channels” and “consumers are getting them confused.” Wade built the Showgirl brand over 12 years vs. Swift’s multi-billion-dollar operation.

Album debuted at No. 1 with 4 million first-week units. Wade’s “Confessions of a Showgirl” trademark registered since 2015. Judge Murillo expressed skepticism about real-world confusion but reserved ruling. Written decision pending.

Story 02 · Lively v. Wayfarer

§ 47.1. Sunday at 2.

The case settled May 4 — but Judge Liman just scheduled oral argument for June 1 at 2 PM on the § 47.1 petition. The key question: who bears the burden of proof? Meanwhile, Blake Brown hair care is hitting clearance shelves.

☆ doc 1437 · may 28 order · judge liman

Burden of proof is the key question.

Judge Liman’s May 28 order (Doc 1437) schedules § 47.1 oral argument for Sunday, June 1 at 2:00 PM. Letter briefs due May 31. The central question: does Baldoni have to prove the anti-SLAPP petition has merit, or does Lively have to prove it doesn’t?

§ 47.1 Oral Argument

June 1 at 2 PM before Judge Liman. Letter briefs due May 31. This is the anti-SLAPP motion — Baldoni wants the case thrown out under California’s free-speech statute, even though it settled. Burden of proof is the pivotal legal question.

Blake Brown · Clearance

Story 04 · Faust v. Paramount & Skydance

Who runs Hollywood now.

The DOJ is reportedly about to greenlight the biggest entertainment merger in history. Five subscribers are trying to stop it. And the new bosses are already cutting projects.

★ $110 billion · doj reportedly approving · closing july 15

The $110 billion deal.

Paramount is buying Warner Bros. Discovery for $110 billion. If it closes on schedule (July 15), one company will control Paramount+, HBO Max, CNN, Discovery+, and roughly a quarter of all streaming subscribers in the U.S. WBD shareholders already voted yes (April 23). Ukraine’s antitrust committee approved. And as of today, U.S. regulators are reportedly likely to approve.

The Lawsuit · Faust v. Paramount

Five Paramount+ subscribers are suing under the Clayton Act — the same antitrust law that broke up the old studio system in the 1948 Paramount decree. Their lawyer is Jeffrey Kessler, who just won the Live Nation monopoly verdict. Private antitrust suits can survive even if the DOJ clears the deal.

Why It Matters

Post-merger: ~23.6% market share in streaming. $49 billion in added debt. House Democrats demanding David Ellison disclose White House communications re: CNN coverage. Freedom of Press Foundation flagging concerns. FCC foreign investment review started May 5.

★ the reynolds connection · the new guard

New bosses. New rules.

The Reynolds Connection

Ryan Reynolds has a first-look deal with Paramount through Dec 2026 (Maximum Effort). Under new Skydance leadership, they already dropped 3 of his projects — Area 51, a Ferris Bueller spin-off, and Eloise. While his wife is locked in Lively v. Wayfarer, Reynolds’ standing at the studio is shifting under new management.

Who’s Running Things

David Ellison (CEO), Jeff Shell (president, ex-NBCUniversal), and Josh Greenstein — who left his job as president of Sony’s film division to co-chair Paramount Pictures. Greenstein started his career at Paramount doing marketing, where he first met Ellison when Skydance co-financed Mission: Impossible. Full circle.

★ star bar tea · quinn emanuel sanctioned $3M · alex spiro’s firm

Quinn Emanuel benchslapped.

Quinn Emanuel Urquhart & Sullivan — home to Alex Spiro, attorney to Jay-Z and Elon Musk — just got hit with $3 million in sanctions plus a $100K punitive fine by Judge Edward M. Chen (N.D. Cal.) in Guardant Health v. Natera.

What They Did

Submitted a supplemental expert report six weeks before trial containing data obtained through questionable means. Made misleading statements to the court about how and when the information was obtained. Judge Chen called it “a culture of lawyering that is deeply disturbing.”

The Sanctions

$3M compensatory to plaintiff + $100K punitive fine. Partners Bramhall & Cannon: up to $58K each. Associate Wang: $28K. Plus the firm must develop and complete an 8-hour legal ethics course — they have to write their own curriculum.

“These attorneys turned a blind eye to the truth, deliberately failed to exercise diligence, violated their duties of candor to the Court, and then attempted to justify it — without basis.”
